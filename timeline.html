History of the Central Process Unit (CPU)

  • ENIAC 1945
    The Electronic Numerical Integrator and Computer (ENIAC) was the world's first general-purpose electronic digital computer.
  • IBM 701 1952
    The IBM 701, or Defense Calculator, was IBM's first commercially available scientific computer.
  • Transistor-based CPUs Late 1950s
    Transition from vacuum tubes to transistors marked a major advancement.
  • IBM System/360 1964
    The System/360 family of computers introduced compatibility across models.
  • Intel 4004 1971
    The Intel 4004, the first microprocessor, marked the birth of the CPU as a single-chip solution.
  • Intel 8086 1978
    The 8086 was Intel's first x86 microprocessor, shaping the architecture for numerous CPUs in the years to come.
  • Motorola 68000 1979
    The 68000 series processors from Motorola were widely used in early personal computers and gaming consoles.
  • Intel 80386 1985
    The 80386 introduced 32-bit architecture, supporting virtual memory and paving the way for modern operating systems.
  • Intel Pentium 1993
    The Pentium processor marked a shift to superscalar architecture, allowing it to execute multiple instructions per clock cycle.
  • AMD Athlon 1999
    AMD's Athlon series introduced innovations like a high-speed system bus and on-die L2 cache.
  • Multi-Core Processors Early 2000s
    The move to multi-core processors became prominent, enhancing parallel processing capabilities.
  • Intel Core Architecture 2006
    The Intel Core processors brought improved performance and energy efficiency.
  • Ryzen Series by AMD 2017
    AMD's Ryzen processors reinvigorated competition, offering strong performance and value.
  • Apple Silicon 2020
    Apple's transition from Intel processors to custom-designed Apple Silicon marked a significant shift.
